üîí Information Security Fundamentals: The CIA Triad

What is the CIA Triad?

The CIA Triad forms the foundation of information security, representing three core principles that guide security policies and implementations. These principles are interconnected and must work together to ensure comprehensive protection.

üîê Confidentiality

Protecting information from unauthorized access

‚úÖ Integrity

Ensuring data accuracy and trustworthiness

‚ö° Availability

Guaranteeing access to authorized users

üéØ Real-World Applications

Banking Example:

  • Confidentiality: Customer account numbers encrypted and access-controlled
  • Integrity: Transaction amounts cannot be modified without authorization
  • Availability: ATM systems operational 24/7 for customer access

üë• Hacker Classifications

Understanding Hacker Motivations

Hackers are classified based on their intent, consent from targets, and legal status. The "hat" terminology originates from old Western movies where protagonists wore white hats and antagonists wore black hats.

‚ö™ White Hat Hackers

(Ethical Hackers)

Intent: Improve security

Consent: Always authorized

Legal Status: Legal


Activities:

  • Penetration testing
  • Vulnerability assessments
  • Security consulting
  • Bug bounty programs

Famous Examples: Charlie Miller, Dan Kaminsky

‚ö´ Black Hat Hackers

(Malicious Hackers)

Intent: Personal gain, harm

Consent: No authorization

Legal Status: Illegal


Activities:

  • Data theft
  • Ransomware attacks
  • Financial fraud
  • System destruction

Example: Kevin Mitnick (before reformation)

üîò Gray Hat Hackers

(Ethical Ambiguity)

Intent: Expose vulnerabilities

Consent: Often no prior authorization

Legal Status: Legal gray area


Activities:

  • Unauthorized penetration
  • Responsible disclosure
  • Public vulnerability announcements
  • Sometimes request payment

Note: Actions may be illegal despite good intentions

‚öîÔ∏è Cyber Kill Chain Methodology

üéØ Understanding the Attack Lifecycle

Developed by Lockheed Martin in 2011, the Cyber Kill Chain provides a framework for understanding and defending against sophisticated cyberattacks. It breaks down attacks into seven distinct phases, allowing defenders to identify and interrupt attacks at multiple points.

1. Reconnaissance

Attackers gather information about the target through passive and active techniques

2. Weaponization

Creating attack vectors like malware, exploits, or payload delivery mechanisms

3. Delivery

Transmitting the weapon to the target via email, websites, or removable media

4. Exploitation

Triggering the vulnerability and executing malicious code on the target system

5. Installation

Installing persistent access mechanisms like backdoors or rootkits

6. Command & Control (C2)

Establishing communication channels for remote system control

7. Actions on Objectives

Achieving the ultimate goal: data exfiltration, system destruction, or espionage

üõ°Ô∏è Defensive Strategies

For each phase, defenders can implement specific countermeasures:

  • Detect: Identify intrusion attempts through monitoring and analysis
  • Deny: Prevent information disclosure and unauthorized access
  • Disrupt: Stop or modify attacker communications
  • Degrade: Counter-attack command and control infrastructure
  • Deceive: Interfere with attacker decision-making through deception
  • Destroy: Eliminate attacker capabilities

üéØ MITRE ATT&CK Framework

üîç Understanding ATT&CK

ATT&CK stands for Adversarial Tactics, Techniques, and Common Knowledge. Developed by MITRE Corporation, it's a globally accessible knowledge base of adversary tactics and techniques based on real-world observations.

üéØ Key Components:

  • Tactics: The "why" - adversary's tactical goals
  • Techniques: The "how" - methods to achieve tactical goals
  • Procedures: Specific implementations of techniques

üìä Enterprise ATT&CK Matrix

The Enterprise matrix contains 14 tactics covering the complete attack lifecycle:

Reconnaissance

Information gathering

Resource Development

Establishing resources

Initial Access

Getting into networks

Execution

Running malicious code

Persistence

Maintaining access

Privilege Escalation

Gaining higher permissions

Defense Evasion

Avoiding detection

Credential Access

Stealing credentials

Discovery

System exploration

Lateral Movement

Moving through networks

Collection

Gathering information

Command & Control

Communicating with systems

Exfiltration

Stealing data

Impact

Manipulating or destroying

üîó Kill Chain vs. ATT&CK

Complementary Frameworks:

  • Kill Chain: High-level, linear progression of attack phases
  • ATT&CK: Detailed, granular techniques that can occur in any order
  • Together: Provide comprehensive attack understanding and defense strategies
